# Product DNA And Motif Ledger

## Purpose

Keep a product coherent without making every page look related by copy-and-paste.

## DNA Audit

Extract stable system qualities:

| Dimension | Questions |
|---|---|
| Typography | What creates voice, hierarchy, and reading rhythm? |
| Color | Which colors are semantic, branded, atmospheric, or merely decorative? |
| Material | Is the product glassy, tactile, editorial, clinical, playful, industrial, or flat? |
| Space | Is density compact, generous, layered, edge-driven, or centered? |
| Motion | Does motion feel calm, elastic, mechanical, organic, immediate, or ceremonial? |
| Tone | Is copy warm, direct, enigmatic, clinical, operational, or conversational? |
| Controls | Which shapes, focus states, and interaction conventions are systemic? |
| Navigation | Which orientation and route patterns must remain predictable? |

Write each finding as a rule, not an adjective. Example: “Primary actions use one warm accent and never compete with status color,” not “premium gold.”

## Motif Ledger

Record every major surface:

| Surface | Information model | Dominant geometry | Artifact | Spatial grammar | Signature interaction | Time behavior |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Example A | sequence | folded pages | active folio | centered depth | turn/unfold | phase shift |
| Example B | collection | vertical spines | task shelf | edge-driven | select/extract | accumulation |

Before accepting a new concept, compare it to the ledger. It must differ from the closest surface on at least three structural axes.

## Preserve Versus Repeat

Preserve:

- type family and hierarchy logic;
- semantic color meaning;
- material quality and lighting restraint;
- navigation behavior;
- motion temperament;
- spacing and interaction quality;
- brand voice.

Do not repeat by default:

- centerpiece silhouette;
- metaphor;
- layout skeleton;
- artifact class;
- signature transition;
- label pattern;
- decorative constellation, line, circle, shelf, book, orb, rail, or card treatment.

Repeat a motif only when users should understand it as the same control with the same behavior across screens.

## Collision Tests

### Blur Test

Blur until text disappears. If the new screen has the same silhouette and focal structure as another page, redesign the composition.

### Logo-Swap Test

Replace the logo and accent color mentally. If the screen could belong unchanged to unrelated software, strengthen the domain-specific information model and artifact.

### Motif-Collision Test

Compare the new surface with its three closest siblings. Reject it when two or more share the same dominant geometry, artifact class, reading path, and interaction.

### DNA-Distance Test

Remove the logo. If the screen no longer feels related to the product, restore typography, material, motion, tone, and navigation—not the old page metaphor.

## Cross-Product Use

For a new product with no existing system, establish provisional DNA from:

- user context and emotional requirement;
- category expectations worth keeping;
- category clichés worth rejecting;
- content density;
- platform conventions;
- accessibility constraints.

Treat the first approved surface as a seed, not a template. Subsequent screens should inherit rules, not layouts.
